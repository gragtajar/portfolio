## Section-by-Section Teardown

### 1. Header & Profile

**What's there:**
> "7 years of product design experience across B2C, SaaS & retail, leading end-to-end UX for mobile, iPad POS & omnichannel systems. Collaborative, calm & outcome-driven."

**The problem:** This reads like a template. "Collaborative, calm & outcome-driven" — these are personality adjectives, not differentiators. Every designer in India describes themselves as collaborative and outcome-driven. It's the UX equivalent of "hard-working team player" on a fresher's resume.

**What's missing:**
- No specific domain claim. Are you a B2B SaaS specialist? A retail-tech designer? An omnichannel expert? Pick a lane and own it.
- No headline-level impact statement. Compare yours to: *"Product designer who reduced in-store wait times by 38% across 1,600 Lenskart stores and took a document management SaaS from zero to commercial launch."* Same person, completely different energy.
- "B2C, SaaS & retail" — this is too scattered. At 7 years, a hiring manager at Razorpay wants to see focus, not breadth. Breadth is a junior signal.

**Score: 2/5**

### 3. Lenskart — Senior Product Designer (Sep 2022 – Sep 2025)

**What's there:** 5 bullet points covering omnichannel ownership, the eye test redesign, contextual inquiry, lens discovery redesign, and junior designer mentorship.

**What's good:**
- The eye test bullet is your single best line: *"Led design of in-store eye test journey for 1600+ stores that reduced wait time by 38% & increased sales conversions by 2.7% in Q2 2023."* This passes the "So what?" test. It has scope (1600+ stores), method (led design), and measurable outcome (38% reduction, 2.7% conversion increase). This is the standard every other bullet on your resume should meet.
- "Owning end-to-end experience for Lenskart's Omni-channel pod" — good scope signal.
- Mentioning contextual inquiry, store visits, UX audits — signals research rigor.

**What's weak:**
- *"Redesigned lens discovery on customer mobile app & iPad POS to reduce drop-offs caused by unclear compatibility of eyeframes & powered lenses."* — Where's the number? Did drop-offs reduce by 5% or 50%? Without a metric, this is just a task description.
- *"Reviewed work of junior designers on user flow, systems thinking & visual layout."* — This is the weakest bullet here. "Reviewed work" is passive. Did you establish a critique process? Mentor them through a shipping cycle? Improve the team's output quality? This reads like you attended review meetings, not that you led anything.
- **The case study link ("Read case study") is a nice touch**, but it only works if the hiring manager actually clicks. The resume itself needs to stand alone.

**What's completely missing from the resume but exists in your case study:**

- The primary/secondary customer profile innovation — your original suggestion that reduced repeat queue-joining. This shows product thinking, not just design execution.
- The design system updates for iPad-first interfaces — signals systems thinking at scale.
- The specific research methods in depth — CCTV analysis via Tango AI, affinity mapping, feature priority matrix with PM.

Your case study is significantly stronger than your resume. The resume is a watered-down version of what you actually did.

**Score: 3.5/5**

## The 5 Biggest Problems (Ranked by Damage)

### 1. The LambdaTest Black Hole
Your most recent, most senior role is empty. This is resume malpractice. Even 2 well-written bullets would transform the top of your resume. Right now, the first thing a Razorpay or Atlassian recruiter sees is... nothing.

### 2. You Bury Your Best Work
The patent-pending prescription sync system, the zero-to-one dox2U launch, the design system contributions, your Figma plugin — none of this is on the resume. Your case study is 3x more compelling than your resume. The resume reads like you're a competent executor. The case study reads like you're a strategic thinker who innovates. These are two different candidates, and you're sending the weaker one to interviews.

### 3. One Metric Is Not a Pattern
The 38% / 2.7% Lenskart stat is excellent. But it's the only quantified outcome on the entire page. A hiring manager needs to believe you consistently deliver measurable impact, not that you got lucky once. dox2U, Apporio, lens discovery redesign, LambdaTest — all metric-free. Even directional metrics ("reduced support tickets," "improved task completion rate") are better than silence.

### 4. Generic Language Disease
Phrases like "proactively collaborated," "drove design initiatives," "iterated quickly on feedback" are filler. They describe process, not outcomes. They make you sound like every other mid-level designer in Gurgaon. At senior/lead level, hiring managers want to see *what you decided, why, and what happened because of it.*

### 5. No Growth Narrative
Your resume lists jobs. It doesn't tell a story. There's no visible arc from "UI/UX designer doing agency work" to "senior product designer owning omnichannel systems at scale." The progression is there in reality — Apporio (agency breadth) → dox2U (zero-to-one ownership) → Lenskart (scale + impact) → LambdaTest (AI frontier) — but you haven't connected the dots for the reader.
